---
title: Don't Specialize. Hybridize.
date: 2024-09-14
---

The standard career advice is to pick a lane and go deep. Specialize. Become the expert. There's a logic to it — expertise is legible, hireable, and compounds over time. The problem is that it optimizes for being predictable in a world that rewards being surprising.

Steph Ango [makes the case for hybridization](https://stephango.com/hybridize) instead. Not generalism — which often means being shallow across many things — but genuine depth in two or more distinct domains, especially domains that don't obviously belong together.

The analogy he uses is materials science. Composite materials and alloys derive their useful properties from the combination of dissimilar elements. Carbon fiber is carbon and epoxy resin. Steel is iron and carbon. Neither component alone has the properties of the result. Hybrid people work the same way.

---

## Two shapes

There are two ways this tends to manifest.

**T-shaped** is the more common framing: a broad base of general competence, with one deep vertical. An engineer who can read a P&L, a designer who can write production code, a data scientist who can run a customer interview. The overlap between disciplines is where T-shaped people add disproportionate value — they can translate across teams, spot where handoffs break down, and lead without losing the technical thread.

**U-shaped** is rarer and stranger: two deep verticals that don't obviously connect. An engineer who trains competitive dance. A doctor who builds hardware. A philosopher who ships software. These combinations feel accidental but they're not — or at least, they don't have to be. The unusual juxtaposition is exactly the source of the insight. Problems that look hard from inside one discipline sometimes look trivially solvable from inside another. The cognitive distance between the two domains is the feature, not the bug.

---

## Why this matters more now

The argument for hybridization has gotten stronger as AI gets better at narrow tasks. If the value you offer is purely executing within a well-defined domain, that's the part that's easiest to automate. What's harder to automate is the ability to see a connection that nobody else sees because nobody else is standing at the intersection you're standing at.

The other thing: complex systems — organizations, codebases, supply chains, cities — tend to fail at the boundaries between disciplines, not at the centers. The mechanical engineer and the electrical engineer each designed their subsystem correctly; the problem is in the interface between them. Hybrid people are disproportionately useful at exactly those seams.

---

## The practical version

You probably already have more than one domain of genuine competence. The question is whether you're treating the combination as an asset or an apology.

Most people in technical fields who have a non-technical background spend energy minimizing it — hedging, omitting it from introductions, treating it as irrelevant. This is backwards. The historian who learned to code isn't a second-rate engineer; they're a first-rate engineer who understands how institutions fail over time, which turns out to be relevant to almost everything.

The combination you have is yours specifically. That's the point. Figure out what only you can see from where you're standing, and go say it.
