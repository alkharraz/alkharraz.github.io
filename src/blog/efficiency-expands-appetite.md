---
title: Efficiency Expands Appetite
date: 2026-04-01
---

There's a recurring anxiety in every age of progress: that the new machine will hollow out work and leave people behind. It hasn't happened yet. Not with the loom, not with the tractor, not with the computer. And it won't happen with AI either.

Human desire doesn't have a ceiling. When something becomes easier to do, we don't do less of it — we do more. Speed a platform up by ten percent, and people spend *more* time on it. Give farmers tractors, and they don't retire — they cultivate more land. Efficiency expands appetite. Economists call this the Jevons paradox — the counterintuitive truth that technological efficiency tends to increase total consumption, not reduce it.

AI follows the same logic. Yes, it will displace certain tasks. But displacement is not disappearance. It is redistribution — of effort, of attention, of possibility. History shows us that every wave of automation that threatened to eliminate work instead transformed it, creating roles and industries that didn't exist before. The pie doesn't stay the same size; it grows.

Think about what happened with the internet. It didn't kill retail — it created e-commerce, logistics networks, and digital marketing at a scale nobody anticipated. It didn't kill journalism — it created a publishing ecosystem where anyone with a voice can reach a global audience. AI will follow a similar arc, opening up possibilities that are genuinely hard to imagine from where we stand today.

What matters isn't whether AI outperforms a human at a specific task. What matters is whether the abundance it creates is shared, and whether the systems we build distribute that growth by design rather than by accident. That is a political and social challenge — not a technological one.

There will be more to do than ever before. The question is whether we're ready to do it.
