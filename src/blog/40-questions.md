---
title: 40 Questions Worth Asking Yourself Every Year
date: 2025-12-28
---

Most people end the year by accident — a midnight countdown, a vague sense that something should feel different, and then January arrives identical to December. Year-end reflection is one of those things everyone agrees is valuable and almost nobody actually does with any rigor.

Steph Ango [publishes a list of 40 questions](https://stephango.com/40-questions) he asks himself every year. The idea is simple: answer them once, then answer them again next year, and the year after that. What you're building isn't a single snapshot — it's a longitudinal record. The trends that emerge over years are more interesting than any individual answer.

I've adapted them here with some commentary. These aren't therapy prompts — they're forensic. You're reconstructing the year from evidence before memory smooths it out.

---

## The questions

**On the year itself**

1. What did you do this year that you'd never done before?
2. Did you keep your new year's resolutions?
3. What cities, countries, or places did you visit?
4. What date from this year will remain etched in your memory, and why?
5. What was your biggest achievement?
6. What was your biggest failure?
7. What other hardships did you face?
8. Did you suffer illness or injury?
9. Compared to this time last year — are you happier or sadder? Richer or poorer? Healthier or unhealthier?

**On people**

10. Did anyone close to you give birth?
11. Did anyone close to you die?
12. Whose behavior merited celebration?
13. Whose behavior made you appalled?
14. Did you fall in love this year?
15. Do you hate anyone now that you didn't hate this time last year?
16. Who did you miss?
17. Who was the best new person you met?

**On money and things**

18. Where did most of your money go?
19. What was the best thing you bought?
20. What did you want and get?
21. What did you want and not get?

**On what you consumed**

22. What was the best book you read?
23. What was your greatest musical discovery?
24. What song will always remind you of this year?
25. What was your favorite film?
26. What was your favorite meal?
27. What was your favorite show?

**On what you felt**

28. What did you get really, really excited about?
29. What do you wish you'd done more of?
30. What do you wish you'd done less of?
31. What kept you sane?
32. What one thing would have made your year immeasurably more satisfying?
33. What would you like to have next year that you lacked this year?

**On the peripheral stuff**

34. How did you spend your birthday?
35. How are you spending the holidays?
36. How would you describe your personal fashion this year?
37. Which public figure did you admire most?
38. What political issue stirred you most?

**On the bottom line**

39. What valuable life lesson did you learn this year?
40. What quote sums up your year?

## How to actually do this

The friction is the enemy. Don't open a blank document at 11pm on December 31st. Set an hour aside before the year ends — somewhere quiet, with a drink, no notifications. Write in whatever tool you'll still have access to in five years. Plain text files outlive every app.

The [MetaFilter thread on year-end rituals](https://ask.metafilter.com/254216/What-are-your-tools-and-rituals-for-reflecting-on-the-past-year) surfaces a few patterns worth stealing: some people review their calendar month by month to jog memory before answering; some review their photos chronologically; some revisit last year's answers first and score themselves. The calendar walk is underrated — you've forgotten more of this year than you think, and the calendar is a receipt.

A few things that make the answers more useful over time:

- **Be specific.** "I traveled more" is useless. "Three weeks in Saudi Arabia, one in Bahrain, and a long weekend in Istanbul where I got mildly lost" is something you can actually read back in ten years.
- **Don't self-censor.** Nobody's reading this. The appalling behavior question has an answer. Write it down.
- **Keep the file.** The value compounds. One year of answers is a diary entry. Ten years of answers is a self-portrait.

The questions aren't the point. The habit is the point.
